Masquerading is when attackers disguise their malicious activities to look like legitimate system processes or user activities. This helps them avoid detection by blending in with normal traffic. Subvert Trust Controls involves undermining the mechanisms that establish trust within a system, such as altering credentials or compromising authentication processes.

So, after successfully evading defenses using these methods, the next logical step would be to gather more information about the internal network and systems to plan their next moves. This could involve understanding what processes are running, what systems are present, and perhaps even accessing sensitive data like password databases.

Looking at the options:

A: T1563 - Remote Service Session Hijacking. This technique involves taking over legitimate user sessions on remote services. While this could be a way to gain access to systems, it seems a bit removed from the immediate goal of gathering internal system information. It might be more relevant later in the attack chain.

B: T1057 - Process Discovery. This involves actions that allow adversaries to get information about running processes on a system. This could be useful for understanding the environment, identifying security software, or finding processes to exploit.

C: T1003.003 - NTDS. This refers to stealing password hashes from the NetNTLMv2 authentication protocol or accessing the NTDS.dit file on a domain controller to obtain password hashes. This seems more targeted towards gaining access to other systems via credential theft rather than general information gathering.

D: T1018 - Remote System Discovery. This technique is about identifying and mapping systems on a network that the adversary can interact with or exploit.

Considering the sequence, after evading defenses, the threat actors would likely want to map out the internal network and identify potential targets. This would involve discovering what systems are present and what services they are running. So, Remote System Discovery seems like a logical next step.

However, Process Discovery also makes sense because understanding what processes are running on a system can provide valuable information about the environment, including security measures in place and potential vulnerabilities to exploit.

Between these two, I think Remote System Discovery is more aligned with gathering internal system information in the context of mapping the network. Process Discovery is more focused on the individual system level, whereas Remote System Discovery helps in understanding the broader network landscape.

NTDS seems a bit more specific and targeted towards credential theft, which might be a step further in the attack chain after they have already mapped the network.

Remote Service Session Hijacking seems even less relevant at this stage, as it's more about taking over existing sessions, which might not directly help in gathering initial system information.

Therefore, I believe the most plausible next step for the threat actors, after evading defenses, would be to engage in Remote System Discovery to map out the internal network and identify potential targets or resources they can exploit.

**Final Answer: D: T1018-Remote System Discovery**
